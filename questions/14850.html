A 42-year-old woman is hospitalized for surgical removal of a mediastinal mass. The patient has had intermittent episodes of double vision, difficulty chewing food, and weakness of the extremities for the past several months. During evaluation she was found to have a circumscribed anterior mediastinal mass. Preoperative nerve conduction studies demonstrated a decremental response to repetitive motor nerve stimulation. The patient has no other medical conditions and does not use tobacco, alcohol, or recreational drugs. She has no drug allergies. Compared to a healthy individual, which of the following anesthesia-related medications is most likely to have increased potency in this patient?

A. Atropine
B. Isoflurane
C. Midazolam
D. Neostigmine
E. Vecuronium

    • Explanation

    This patient has an anterior mediastinal mass and a nerve conduction study consistent with myasthenia gravis (MG), a disease caused by autoantibodies against postsynaptic nicotinic acetylcholine receptors found within the neuromuscular junction. Antibody-induced degradation leads to fewer functional receptors, which may be insufficient to generate muscle action potentials despite normal acetylcholine release.

    Weakness most commonly affects the extraocular, bulbar, and proximal muscles, worsening with repetitive nerve stimulation (fatigability) due to depletion of acetylcholine stores in the presynaptic nerve terminal. Thymic abnormalities (eg, thymoma, thymic hyperplasia) are common and may appear as an anterior mediastinal mass on imaging; thymectomy can often improve symptoms.

    Neuromuscular blocking agents are used to induce skeletal muscle relaxation during surgery by competing with acetylcholine at the postsynaptic membrane. They can be subclassified by their effects:

    • Nondepolarizing agents (eg, vecuronium, rocuronium) are competitive antagonists of nicotinic acetylcholine receptors. Because of the low number of functioning receptors, patients with MG are extremely sensitive to these agents; very small doses can induce paralysis with loss of airway patency.
    • Depolarizing agents (eg, succinylcholine) are competitive agonists of nicotinic acetylcholine receptors; persistent depolarization leads to desensitization of the motor end plate. Because the low number of functioning receptors tends to inhibit end-plate depolarization, patients with MG are often resistant to these agents; extremely large doses are required to induce muscle relaxation.

    (Choice A) Atropine is a muscarinic acetylcholine receptor antagonist often used before general anesthesia to decrease salivary and bronchial secretions (helping maintain airway patency) and to prevent myocardial depression (minimizing risk for hypotension). It has no effect on nicotinic acetylcholine receptors in the neuromuscular junctions.

    (Choices B and C) Isoflurane is a general anesthetic (acting centrally on multiple receptors) that can cause malignant hyperthermia in those with genetic predispositions. Midazolam is a benzodiazepine (acting on the GABA A receptor) that produces sedation. These medications are not contraindicated in MG.

    (Choice D) Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (eg, neostigmine, pyridostigmine) hinder the degradation of acetylcholine in the synapse, thereby increasing the amount available to stimulate nicotinic receptors. They are often used to hasten recovery from nondepolarizing neuromuscular blockade and to improve muscle strength in patients with MG. Because such patients have fewer functional receptors, acetylcholinesterase inhibitors typically exhibit decreased potency.

    Educational objective:

    Myasthenia gravis (MG) is caused by autoantibodies against postsynaptic nicotinic acetylcholine receptors, leading to fewer functional receptors and fatigable muscle weakness. Nondepolarizing neuromuscular blocking agents (eg, vecuronium) are competitive antagonists of nicotinic receptors; because of the depletion of receptors, patients with MG are extremely sensitive to these agents.
