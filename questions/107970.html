A 56-year-old man comes to the office due to feeling "dizzy" over the past 2 weeks. He works in a warehouse and says he experiences a brief, severe spinning sensation when looking up at objects on a high shelf. Vital signs are normal. Examination shows normal hearing. Ocular movements in a sitting position are normal, but when the patient's head is rotated to the right side off the examination table, he develops vertigo and rotatory nystagmus. The remainder of the examination shows no abnormalities. Which of the following is the most likely cause of this patient's symptoms?

A. Carotid baroreceptor dysfunction
B. Elevated endolymphatic hydrostatic pressure
C. Elevated hydrostatic pressure in the middle ear
D. Loose semicircular canal debris
E. Transient cerebellar hypoperfusion

    • Explanation

    This patient has vertigo, often described as a spinning (as in this patient), whirling, or tilting sensation that can be due to an underlying disease in either the peripheral or central nervous system. This patient's brief (<1min), episodic vertigo triggered by head position changes (eg, sitting up from bed, looking around a kitchen) is consistent with benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV), the most common cause of vertigo.

    Rotational head movement is sensed by the semicircular canals, which are filled with endolymphatic fluid. The relative movement of this fluid stimulates hair cells that generate electrical impulses which are transmitted to the brain. BPPV is most often attributed to canalithiasis, or the presence of crystalline debris (canaliths) in the semicircular canals. These canaliths disrupt the normal flow of fluid in the semicircular canals, leading to contradictory signaling from the corresponding canals.

    Diagnosis is based on clinical features. The Dix-Hallpike maneuver can help diagnose BPPV: vertigo and nystagmus are triggered as the patient quickly lies back into a supine position with the head rotated 45 degrees. No further workup is needed in patients with classic presentations. Treatment with canalith repositioning maneuvers (eg, Epley) is typically effective.

    (Choices A and E) Carotid baroreceptor dysfunction can lead to wide fluctuations in blood pressure. Hypoperfusion due to this or other causes (eg, arterial stenosis) can lead to lightheadedness, which is frequently interpreted by patients as dizziness. However, this patient's positive Dix-Hallpike and spinning sensation (vs lightheadedness) are more consistent with BPPV.

However, acute otitis media presents with pain, fevers, and hearing loss, features absent in this patient.

    Educational objective:

    Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo causes brief (<1 min) episodes of vertigo triggered by changes in head position. Triggering an episode by using provocative head positioning maneuvers (eg, Dix-Hallpike) is diagnostic.
