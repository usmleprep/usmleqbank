A 25-year-old primigravida at 18 weeks gestation comes to the office with her husband for routine follow-up. She reports no concerns, and abdominal examination shows normal fundal height. Anatomy ultrasound of the fetus shows male sex. The fetus' kidneys appear fused at the lower poles and are located low in the abdomen. The remainder of the ultrasound is unremarkable. The couple asks about implications of these findings and the ways they will affect the child after birth. Which of the following is the best response?

A. "Many children with this disorder develop kidney dysfunction that progresses to end-stage renal disease, so we will need to follow up closely for this."
B. "Patients with these findings are at an increased risk for forming cerebral aneurysms over their lifetime, so periodic screening is recommended."
C. "The majority of patients with this developmental anomaly are asymptomatic, but there is an increased risk for urinary tract infections."
D. "These findings are part of a developmental sequence that can lead to impaired lung development with potential respiratory distress at delivery."
E. "This genetic condition is associated with varying degrees of liver disease, which warrants periodic imaging of the liver."

    • Explanation

    This patient's prenatal ultrasound reveals a fetus with fusion of the inferior kidney poles, a congenital anomaly known as horseshoe kidney.

    Pathophysiology of horseshoe kidney involves abnormal migration of the right and left metanephric blastema (from which the renal parenchyma is derived), leading to fusion of the developing inferior poles. The kidneys' physiologic ascent into the abdomen is limited due to the fused parenchyma (isthmus), which is mechanically restricted by the overlying inferior mesenteric artery (IMA) at its origin from the aorta (low-lying position).

    Most patients with horseshoe kidney and its associated anatomic abnormalities are asymptomatic. However, in some patients, the abnormal position of the kidney and ureters may lead to intermittent ureteropelvic junction obstruction and urinary stasis, which increase the risk for nephroliths and urinary tract infections.

    (Choices A and D) Patients with autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease (ARPKD) often have decreased renal function in utero, which may lead to oligohydramnios and pulmonary hypoplasia (Potter sequence). ARPKD also frequently leads to end-stage kidney disease, warranting close monitoring. However, prenatal ultrasound would reveal large, hyperechogenic kidneys with many tiny cysts in contrast to this patient's fused kidney.

    (Choices B and E) Both ARPKD and autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease (ADPKD) are associated with hepatic manifestations, specifically bile duct dysgenesis and fibrosis in ARPKD and asymptomatic cysts in ADPKD. Patients with ADPKD are also at risk for cerebral aneurysms due to abnormal gene expression in vascular smooth muscle cells. Horseshoe kidney is not associated with extrarenal manifestations (eg, liver disease, cerebral aneurysms).

    Educational objective:

    Horseshoe kidney is a congenital anomaly in which the inferior poles of the developing kidney fuse in utero. Most patients remain asymptomatic but are at increased risk for recurrent nephroliths and urinary tract infections due to the abnormal orientation and positioning of the kidney.
