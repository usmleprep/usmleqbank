A 21-year-old man comes to the emergency department due to an episode of syncope. The syncopal episode was not provoked by any activity or circumstance and was not preceded by lightheadedness. The patient has no significant medical history and does not take any medications. ECG demonstrates a prolonged QT interval but is otherwise unremarkable. Assuming this patient's presentation is due to an inherited condition, the relevant mutation most likely affects which of the following cardiomyocyte components?

A. Lysosomal hydrolytic enzymes
B. Membrane potassium channels

    This patient's sudden-onset syncopal episode in the setting of QT-interval prolongation on ECG is consistent with long QT syndrome (LQTS). In the cardiac action potential, the QT interval corresponds with ventricular repolarization (phase 3), which is primarily controlled by voltage-gated potassium channels. LQTS typically results from a congenital or an acquired (or a combination of both) impairment of these voltage-gated potassium channels. Young and healthy patients who exhibit LQTS most likely have a congenital etiology.

    The 2 most important congenital syndromes that cause QT-interval prolongation are:

    • Romano-Ward syndrome (autosomal dominant, more common)
    • Jervell and Lange-Nielsen syndrome (autosomal recessive, associated with sensorineural hearing loss)

    By impairing the function of voltage-gated potassium channels and slowing ventricular repolarization to prolong the QT interval, these congenital syndromes increase the risk of torsades de pointes, a type of polymorphic ventricular tachycardia that can cause syncope or sudden cardiac death (SCD).

    (Choice A) Mutations affecting lysosomal hydrolytic enzymes can result in lysosomal storage disease; the disease types with prominent cardiac manifestations include Pompe disease and Fabry disease. Affected patients typically develop cardiomyopathy that creates increased risk of ventricular arrhythmia, but QT-interval prolongation is not expected.

    (Choices C and D) Mutations of the cardiac sarcomere contractile proteins beta-myosin heavy chain and myosin-binding protein C are the most common causes of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM). Mutations of the cardiac sarcomere structural protein titin are the most common cause of congenital dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM). Both HCM and DCM increase the risk of ventricular arrhythmia and resulting syncope or SCD, but they are not expected to prolong the QT interval.

    (Choice E) Mutations affecting sarcoplasmic reticulum calcium channels underlie catecholamine-induced polymorphic ventricular tachycardia, which typically presents with exercise- or emotion-induced ventricular arrhythmia. These mutations do not cause any abnormalities on resting ECG (eg, QT interval is normal).

    Educational objective:

    The QT-interval on ECG corresponds with ventricular repolarization, which is primarily controlled by voltage-gated potassium channels. Congenital mutations impairing these potassium channels cause prolongation of the QT interval. Affected patients are at increased risk for torsade de pointes, a type of polymorphic ventricular tachycardia that can cause syncope or sudden cardiac death.
