A 14-year-old girl is brought to the office for a routine physical examination. The patient will be starting her freshman year of high school soon. She plays the clarinet in the school band but does not play any sports. The patient is not sexually active and does not use tobacco, alcohol, or illicit drugs. Height is at the 5th percentile, and weight is at the 25th percentile. Temperature is 36.7 C (98 F), blood pressure is 120/80 mm Hg, pulse is 88/min, and respirations are 16/min. Physical examination shows a short and thick neck, a broad chest, and shortened fourth metacarpals bilaterally. A murmur is heard on cardiac auscultation. Which of the following would most likely be seen on echocardiography?

A. Atrial septal defect
B. Bicuspid aortic valve
C. Mitral stenosis
D. Mitral valve prolapse
E. Patent ductus arteriosus
F. Ventricular septal defect

    • Explanation

    This patient's short stature, short and thick (ie, webbed) neck, broad chest, and shortened fourth metacarpals are characteristic of Turner syndrome. Bicuspid aortic valve is the most common congenital cardiac malformation in patients with Turner syndrome, resulting from fusion of 2 of the 3 valve cusps. It is usually an isolated abnormality; however, it may occur in combination with other anomalies, particularly aortic coarctation.

    A bicuspid aortic valve typically creates an early systolic ejection sound over the right upper sternal border (ie, aortic area), often with a faint ejection murmur (aortic flow murmur). The abnormal structure and altered hemodynamics surrounding the valve (eg, turbulent flow) accelerate age-related calcification compared to a normal tricuspid aortic valve. This progressive calcification commonly leads to valvular dysfunction in the form of aortic stenosis or, less commonly, aortic regurgitation (with the associated murmurs). A bicuspid aortic valve also creates increased risk of infective endocarditis.

    (Choices A and F) Complete atrioventricular canal defect, isolated atrial septal defect (ASD), and isolated ventricular septal defect (VSD) are commonly seen in Down syndrome, but they are not typical of Turner syndrome.

    (Choice C) Mitral stenosis is rarely congenital and most commonly occurs as a result of rheumatic heart disease.

    (Choice D) Mitral valve prolapse is commonly seen in genetic connective tissue disorders, including Marfan syndrome and Ehlers-Danlos syndrome. It is not typically seen with Turner syndrome.

    (Choice E) Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA) is associated with congenital rubella syndrome. It also occurs with increased incidence in Turner syndrome (compared to the general population), but the association is much weaker than that of bicuspid aortic valve. Unlike bicuspid aortic valve that usually remains asymptomatic for decades, PDA typically causes symptoms early in life (ie, heart failure).

    Educational objective:

    Turner syndrome is associated with congenital anomalies of the aorta, most commonly bicuspid aortic valve. A bicuspid aortic valve typically creates an early systolic click over the right upper sternal border, often with a faint ejection murmur. The abnormal structure and altered hemodynamics surrounding the valve accelerate age-related valvular calcification, commonly leading to aortic stenosis or, less commonly, aortic regurgitation.
