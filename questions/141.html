    This patient with exertional chest pain, family history of sudden cardiac death, and a crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur that intensifies on standing most likely has hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM), an autosomal dominant inherited condition involving mutations in cardiac sarcomere proteins. The clinical manifestations of HCM result mostly from enlargement of the intraventricular septum that leads to left ventricular outflow tract (LVOT) obstruction. Patients may be asymptomatic or can experience exertional dyspnea, fatigue, anginal pain, lightheadedness, or syncope; sudden cardiac arrest (or death) can occur due to reduced cardiac output and ventricular arrhythmia.

    The degree of LVOT obstruction in HCM varies with changes in left ventricular (LV) blood volume. Low LV blood volume collapses the LVOT and worsens the obstruction; therefore, medications that decrease LV blood volume should be avoided. These medications include the following:

    • Preload reducers: Venous dilators (eg, nitrates) and diuretics (eg, furosemide) reduce venous return to the heart and decrease LV end-diastolic volume.
    • Afterload reducers: Arterial dilators (eg, hydralazine) facilitate increased stroke volume, which decreases LV end-systolic volume.
    • Balanced vasodilators: Combined venous and arterial dilators (eg, dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers, ACE inhibitors) can worsen LVOT obstruction via reductions in preload and afterload.

    In contrast, negative chronotropic agents (which reduce heart rate, increasing diastolic filling time) and negative inotropic agents (which reduce contractility, decreasing the systolic ejection fraction) help improve LVOT obstruction by increasing LV volumes. Beta blockers (eg, metoprolol) and nondihydropyridine calcium channel blockers (eg, verapamil) have negative chronotropic and inotropic effects and are used for treating HCM (Choices D and E). Disopyramide, a class I antiarrhythmic (ie, sodium channel blocker) that has negative inotropic effects, is also effective via the same mechanism (Choice B).

    (Choice A) Amiodarone is a class III antiarrhythmic (potassium channel blocker) that may be used in patients with HCM who develop arrhythmia complications (eg, atrial fibrillation, ventricular tachycardia).

    Educational objective:

    Left ventricular outflow tract obstruction in hypertrophic cardiomyopathy worsens with decreased left ventricular blood volume. Therefore, medications that decrease preload (eg, nitrates), afterload (eg, hydralazine), or both (eg, dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers, ACE inhibitors) should be avoided.
