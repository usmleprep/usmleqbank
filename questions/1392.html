A 58-year-old man comes to the emergency department with fever, headache, and nausea for the past day. He underwent kidney transplantation last year. His temperature is 39.4 C (103 F), blood pressure is 130/80 mm Hg, pulse is 98/min, and respirations are 16/min. Neck stiffness is present on physical examination. Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) results are as follows:

Glucose 40 mg/dL
Protein 80 mg/dL
Leukocytes 900/mm3
Neutrophils 80%

Gram-positive rods with tumbling motility at room temperature are seen on CSF microscopy. This patient's infection was most likely transmitted through which of the following mechanisms?

A. Arthropod bite
B. Contaminated food
C. Needlestick
D. Respiratory droplets
E. Unprotected sex

    • Explanation

    This patient, who is almost certainly taking anti-rejection immunosuppressants given his kidney transplantation, has fever, headache, nausea, and neck stiffness that are suggestive of bacterial meningitis. His cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) findings (elevated leukocytes with neutrophil predominance, elevated protein, low-normal glucose, presence of bacteria) confirm the diagnosis.

    The microbiologic characteristics are further consistent with Listeria monocytogenes, a facultative intracellular (grows within macrophages), gram-positive rod, with distinctive flagellar-based tumbling motility (typically seen at room temperature but not at human body temperatures). Listeria can multiply in cold temperatures (4-10 C), a unique feature that allows it to grow well in refrigerated foods.

    Listeriosis most often occurs in immunocompromised adults (eg, renal transplantation, hematologic malignancy). The bacteria can access the bloodstream following ingestion of contaminated food (eg, unpasteurized milk and milk products, undercooked meats, unwashed raw vegetables) and cause bacteremia and meningoencephalitis. Listeria can also be transmitted transplacentally or via vaginal contact during delivery, causing neonatal meningitis. Ampicillin is the antibiotic of choice.

    (Choice A) Leishmaniasis (sandfly bite), Plasmodium/malaria (Anopheles mosquito bite), Chagas disease (reduviid bug bite), and Borrelia burgdorferi/Lyme disease (Ixodes tick bite) are all transmitted by arthropod bite. None of these would account for this patient's CSF findings of a gram-positive rod with tumbling motility.

    (Choice C) Needlesticks can transmit HIV, hepatitis B and C, and other infections.

    (Choice D) Neisseria meningitidis (gram-negative diplococcus), Haemophilus influenzae (gram-negative coccobacillus), and Streptococcus pneumoniae (gram-positive diplococcus) are common causes of meningitis transmitted via respiratory droplets. Listeria (gram-positive rod that tumbles at room temperature) is not transmitted in this manner.

    (Choice E) Unprotected sex is the mode of transmission of HIV, hepatitis B, gonorrhea, chlamydia, syphilis, human papillomavirus (a cause of cervical cancer), and others.

    Educational objective:

    Listeriosis is most commonly transmitted through food ingestions and can cause meningitis in immunocompromised adults. Listeria is a gram-positive rod with tumbling motility. It grows well in cold temperatures and can therefore contaminate refrigerated food.
