A 34-year-old woman with polycystic ovary syndrome comes to the office with her husband for treatment of infertility. The patient has been unable to conceive despite having unprotected sexual intercourse several times a week for the past 2 years. Her menses are irregular and occur every 2-3 months, consistent with chronic anovulation. She does not use tobacco, alcohol, or illicit drugs. The patient takes no medications and has no allergies. BMI is 32 kg/m2. Physical examination shows coarse hair on her chin and abdomen. The patient receives ovulation induction therapy with a short course of gonadotropins followed by a single injection of hCG. The use of hCG therapy in this patient primarily mimics the normal physiologic increase in which of the following hormones?

A. Estrogen
B. FSH
C. LH
D. Progesterone
E. Testosterone

    • Explanation

    Reproductive-age women have monthly menstrual cycles (ovulation and bleeding) due to cyclic hormonal changes in the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis (HPO axis). FSH and LH are gonadotropins released by the anterior pituitary. FSH stimulates maturation of a dominant ovarian follicle, which produces estrogen. During the late follicular phase (ie, midcycle), the increasing estrogen levels cause the hypothalamus to release GnRH in a specific pulsatile pattern that triggers the LH surge. The LH surge then induces follicular rupture and oocyte release (ie, ovulation).

    This patient with coarse chin hair likely has anovulatory infertility related to polycystic ovary syndrome. Patients with anovulatory infertility often undergo ovulation induction therapy with exogenous gonadotropins (ie, FSH) to stimulate ovarian follicular development (Choice B). Once the follicles appear mature on ultrasound, patients receive an injection of hCG. The hormones hCG, LH, FSH, and TSH share a common alpha subunit; in addition, the beta subunit of hCG is also structurally similar to the beta subunit of LH. Due to this beta subunit similarity, hCG can bind to and activate the same receptors as LH. Therefore, the hCG injection mimics the LH surge and acts as the ovulation trigger.

    (Choice A) Estrogen, produced by the dominant ovarian follicle, physiologically increases and peaks in the late follicular phase. This change leads to the LH surge, follicular rupture, and ovulation. Estrogen levels decrease with rupture of the dominant follicle.

    (Choice D) After ovulation, the remnants of the dominant follicle form the corpus luteum, which secretes progesterone. Progesterone induces endometrial decidualization in preparation for pregnancy implantation. An implanted pregnancy will secrete hCG as a signal to the ovary to maintain the corpus luteum until the placenta can take over progesterone production. If no pregnancy implants, the corpus luteum regresses and progesterone decreases.

    (Choice E) Testosterone is produced in the adrenal glands and ovarian theca cells. Testosterone production within theca cells increases slightly during the late follicular phase in response to the LH surge.

    Educational objective:

    Anovulatory infertility can be treated with ovulation induction therapy, which may include a course of gonadotropins to stimulate the ovarian follicles. Once the follicles mature, patients are administered an injection of hCG, which mimics the LH surge and acts as the ovulation trigger.
