A 75-year-old man comes to the office due to worsening dyspnea and fatigue on exertion over the past 6 months. Recently, he has had severe lightheadedness during physical activity. Blood pressure is 118/86 mm Hg and pulse is 65/min and regular. Physical examination reveals a harsh ejection-type systolic murmur at the base of the heart radiating to the neck. S2 is diminished in intensity, and an S4 is heard at the cardiac apex. Which of the following is the most likely cause of this patient's heart condition?

A. Extensive valve calcification with impaired leaflet mobility
B. Fusion of valve commissures due to recurrent inflammation
C. Hypertrophy of the basal interventricular septum with subaortic obstruction
D. Infectious vegetations attached to the valve cusps
E. Myxomatous valve degeneration with leaflet prolapse

    This patient with progressive dyspnea and fatigue, exertional lightheadedness (ie, presyncope), and a systolic ejection murmur at the base of the heart (ie, upper sternum) most likely has severe aortic stenosis (AS). Patients with AS typically remain asymptomatic until the stenosis becomes severe and substantially limits cardiac output. Exertional dyspnea is commonly an initial symptom and is often progressively followed by angina, syncope or presyncope, and eventually decompensated heart failure.

    Physical examination in AS reveals a crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur best heard at the right upper sternal border with radiation to the carotid arteries. S2 is typically diminished in intensity due to reduced mobility of the aortic leaflets, and an S4 may be heard due to concentric left ventricular hypertrophy and reduced diastolic compliance.

    In developed countries, the most common cause of AS is age-related calcific degeneration of a normal trileaflet aortic valve, which involves pathophysiology similar to atherosclerosis and typically affects patients age >70. Patients with a congenital bicuspid aortic valve can undergo an accelerated calcification process (likely due to altered hemodynamics of the bicuspid valve) and may develop AS as early as age 40-50.

    (Choice B) Fusion of the valve commissures due to recurrent inflammation occurs in rheumatic heart disease (RHD), which is a common cause of AS worldwide but is rare in developed countries. When AS does develop due to RHD, it usually presents in early adulthood and is accompanied by mitral valve disease.

    (Choice C) Hypertrophy of the basal interventricular septum with subaortic obstruction is present in hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM). The murmur of HCM is similar to the murmur of AS, but it is best heard at the apex or left lower sternal border and does not radiate to the neck. In addition, a normal S2 is expected.

    (Choice D) Infective endocarditis typically leads to valvular regurgitation. Aortic regurgitation causes a decrescendo diastolic murmur, and mitral regurgitation causes a holosystolic murmur.

    (Choice E) Myxomatous degeneration of the mitral valve leaflets leads to mitral valve prolapse. Cardiac auscultation usually reveals a midsystolic click followed by a murmur of mitral regurgitation.

    Educational objective:

    Age-related calcific degeneration of a normal trileaflet aortic valve is the most common cause of aortic stenosis in developed countries, typically affecting patients age >70. Aortic stenosis is recognized by a crescendo-decrescendo murmur best heard at the right upper sternal border with radiation to the carotid arteries. When severe, it causes symptoms (eg, dyspnea on exertion, presyncope) due to restriction of cardiac output.
