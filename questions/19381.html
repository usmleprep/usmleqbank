    CFTR = cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator.

    Cystic fibrosis (CF) is an autosomal recessive disorder in which an abnormality in the CF transmembrane conductance regulator results in the accumulation of thick, viscous mucus in multiple organ systems. In particular, inspissated airway secretions block the respiratory tract and impair mucociliary clearance, leading to bacterial colonization and chronic, recurrent sinopulmonary infections.

    CF pulmonary exacerbations are characterized by a decline in lung function (ie, forced expiratory volume in 1 sec) and new or worsening cough, shortness of breath, or sputum production. Infection and subsequent airway inflammation are typically responsible for the acute change in respiratory status, and common bacterial pathogens include Staphylococcus aureus, nontypeable Haemophilus influenzae, and Pseudomonas aeruginosa. With progressive disease, however, patients are also at risk for colonization with Burkholderia cepacia complex, a fastidious gram-negative bacterium that is often difficult to eradicate and associated with increased morbidity and mortality.

    Sputum cultures are typically performed during CF pulmonary exacerbation to identify the pathogen and guide antimicrobial management. In addition to regular blood (Streptococcus identification), chocolate (H influenzae), and MacConkey (Pseudomonas [forms colorless colonies]) agars, sputum should also be plated on mannitol salt agar (S aureus) and B cepacia‚Äìselective agar.

    (Choices A, C, D, and E) Bordetella pertussis, Corynebacterium, Legionella, Salmonella, and Shigella do not commonly cause CF exacerbations.

    Patients with cystic fibrosis are at risk for pulmonary exacerbations due to Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, nontypeable Haemophilus influenzae, and Burkholderia cepacia complex.
