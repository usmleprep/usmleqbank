A 44-year-old man is brought to the emergency department by his wife due to fever, headache, and confusion for the last 1 day. The patient also has experienced nausea and photophobia. Medical history includes hypertension and type 2 diabetes mellitus. He had an upper respiratory tract illness 2 weeks ago. The patient does not use tobacco, alcohol, or recreational drugs. He lives in Massachusetts and has not traveled recently. Temperature is 38.3 C (101 F). On physical examination, pupillary reflexes and funduscopic findings are normal. Nasal and oropharyngeal mucous membranes are normal. The lungs are clear on auscultation, and heart sounds are unremarkable. Neurologic examination shows nuchal rigidity. No rashes are present. Lumbar puncture is performed, and cerebrospinal fluid analysis results are as follows:

Opening pressure 200 mm H2O
Glucose 20 mg/dL
Protein 200 mg/dL
Red blood cells 4 cells/mm3
Leukocytes 760 cells/mm3
Neutrophils 90%
Lymphocytes 10%

Which of the following best describes the pathogen most likely responsible for this patient's condition?

A. Encapsulated budding yeast
B. Lancet-shaped gram-positive cocci
C. Nonenveloped single-stranded RNA virus
D. Nonmotile gram-negative coccobacilli
E. Tick-transmitted motile spirochete

    • Explanation

    This patient with confusion, headache, fever, and nuchal rigidity likely has meningitis. In bacterial meningitis, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis typically shows elevated opening pressure, increased neutrophils, decreased glucose, and elevated protein. The characteristic appearance of any observed bacteria on CSF Gram stain provides preliminary identification of the pathogen and allows tailoring of antibiotic therapy (whereas cultures take time to grow).

    The most common cause of bacterial meningitis in adults of all ages is Streptococcus pneumoniae, a lancet-shaped gram-positive cocci found in pairs. S pneumoniae meningitis often follows a pulmonary infection or mild upper respiratory tract infection that has weakened ciliary clearance and the epithelial barrier. Patients with immunocompromisation (eg, alcohol use disorder, diabetes mellitus, asplenia) are at greater risk for bacterial meningitis.

    (Choice A) Encapsulated budding yeasts are characteristic of cryptococcal meningitis, a fungal infection primarily seen in those with profound immunocompromise (eg, AIDS). Patients typically have slowly progressive headache, low-grade fever, and confusion over a few weeks (not 1-2 days). Although most cases are marked by low CSF glucose and protein levels, CSF leukocytes are usually mildly elevated and predominantly mononuclear.

    (Choice C) Enteroviruses (nonenveloped single-stranded RNA viruses) are the most common cause of viral (aseptic) meningitis. Although patients with viral meningitis often have sudden-onset meningeal symptoms, CSF usually has normal glucose, mildly elevated protein (eg, to <150 mg/dL), and a leukocyte count <250 cells/mm3.

    (Choice D) Haemophilus influenzae is a gram-negative coccobacillus. H influenzae infection is less common now that vaccination with the Hib capsule vaccine (a protein capsule polysaccharide conjugate vaccine) has become widespread.

    (Choice E) Lyme meningitis, a tick-transmitted spirochetal infection, typically presents with a syndrome similar to viral meningitis. CSF analysis usually shows a lymphocyte-predominant meningitis with mild leukocyte elevation, minimal change in glucose, and slightly elevated protein.

    Educational objective:

    Bacterial meningitis causes an increase in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) neutrophil count and protein concentration as well as a decrease in CSF glucose. Streptococcus pneumoniae infection is a leading cause of community-acquired pneumonia, otitis media, and meningitis in adults. S pneumoniae appears on Gram stain as lancet-shaped gram-positive cocci in pairs.
