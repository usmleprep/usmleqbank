A 30-year-old nulligravid woman and her 31-year-old husband come to the office for an infertility evaluation. They have been unable to conceive despite 2 years of unprotected intercourse. The patient has had irregular periods since menarche, ranging between 30 and 70 days each cycle. Her BMI is 34 kg/m2. Examination shows a small, anteverted uterus and bilateral enlarged ovaries. A urine pregnancy test is negative. The patient is prescribed letrozole for ovulation induction therapy. This medication is most likely to help this patient through which of the following mechanisms?

A. Activation of pituitary dopamine receptors
B. Depletion of hypothalamic estrogen receptors
C. Increased proliferation of endometrial tissue
D. Inhibition of hypothalamic gonadotropin secretion
E. Suppression of ovarian estradiol production

    • Explanation

    This patient with irregular menses and enlarged ovaries likely has polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), a common cause of infertility due to anovulation. Ovulation induction agents (eg, letrozole, clomiphene) can be used to treat anovulatory infertility by increasing gonadotropin levels (ie, FSH, LH), which stimulates ovulation. Letrozole and clomiphene stimulate gonadotropin release via manipulation of different targets on the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian (HPO) axis:

    • Letrozole acts on the ovary. Patients with PCOS typically have increased ovarian estrogen (eg, estradiol) production, which inhibits FSH, LH, and ovulation. Ovarian estrogen production occurs primarily by the conversion of androgens (produced by theca cells) into estrogens within granulosa cells that contain the enzyme aromatase. Letrozole inhibits aromatase (ie, prevents androgen-to-estrogen conversion) and suppresses ovarian estradiol production. Letrozole also inhibits aromatase in adipose tissue, decreasing another source of estrogen (eg, estrone). In response, the pituitary releases more FSH and LH, which stimulates ovulation.
    • Clomiphene acts on estrogen receptors in the hypothalamus and pituitary. When estrogen levels are high (as in PCOS), there is negative estrogenic feedback on both the hypothalamus and pituitary to stop the release of GnRH, FSH, and LH. Clomiphene binds to estrogen receptors for an extended period, resulting in receptor depletion in the hypothalamus and pituitary, thereby allowing for continued FSH and LH release (Choice B).

    (Choice A) Bromocriptine, a dopamine receptor agonist, decreases prolactin secretion. Prolactin normally inhibits FSH release, so patients with hyperprolactinemia (eg, lactotroph adenoma) often have infertility. In these patients, bromocriptine can normalize prolactin levels and restore FSH and estrogen levels.

    (Choice C) Increased endometrial proliferation, which increases the risk for endometrial hyperplasia or cancer, occurs with the use of unopposed estrogen (eg, estrogen-only hormone therapy) or certain selective estrogen receptor modulators (eg, tamoxifen).

    (Choice D) When administered continuously, leuprolide (GnRH agonist) downregulates pituitary GnRH receptors and decreases FSH and estrogen, causing a hypogonadotropic, hypogonadal state clinically similar to that of menopause.

    Educational objective:

    Polycystic ovary syndrome can present with irregular menses, enlarged ovaries, and anovulatory infertility. Letrozole, an aromatase inhibitor, acts on the ovary and peripheral adipose tissue to inhibit androgen-to-estrogen conversion by the enzyme aromatase; the subsequent decrease in estrogen production causes the pituitary to release more FSH and LH, thereby stimulating ovulation.
