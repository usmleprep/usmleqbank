A 24-year-old woman comes to the office for a routine prenatal ultrasound examination. She is 19 weeks pregnant based on her last menstrual period. This is the patient's third pregnancy, and there have been no complications. Family medical history is unremarkable, and both of the patient's children are healthy. The examination reveals a male fetus with bilaterally enlarged kidneys with diffuse small cysts. The amniotic fluid volume is very low. No other anomalies are seen. Which of the following will most likely be present in the newborn at delivery?

A. Bladder outlet obstruction
B. Cerebral aneurysms
C. Facial hemangiomas
D. Intestinal atresia
E. Pulmonary hypoplasia

    • Explanation

    This mother has oligohydramnios, and the fetus has bilaterally enlarged cystic kidneys. Because amniotic fluid is largely composed of fetal urine, these findings are concerning for fetal kidney dysfunction and reduced urine production. In this case, the fetus likely has autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease (ARPKD), a condition caused by a mutation in fibrocystin (protein found in epithelial cells of renal tubules). Fibrocystin deficiency leads to diffuse microcysts, as seen here, and impaired kidney function.

    Any condition that causes anuria or oliguria in utero (eg, ARPKD, bilateral renal agenesis) results in severe oligohydramnios and risk of Potter sequence. Potter sequence is a predictable series of anomalies caused by fetal compression from insufficient amniotic fluid. Findings include the following:

    • Flattened facies: Extrinsic fetal compression leads to a flattened, depressed nasal bridge; prominent epicanthal folds; low-set ears; and a recessed chin.
    • Limb deformities: Restricted fetal movement causes deformities such as contractures, hip dislocation, and clubfoot.
    • Pulmonary hypoplasia: Restricted thoracic expansion limits fetal lung development, causing respiratory distress and decreased breath sounds after birth. Amniotic fluid is also thought to promote production of elastin, collagen, and vascular endothelial growth factor in fetal lungs, and oligohydramnios likely results in reduced alveolarization and angiogenesis.

    Potter sequence is often fatal in infancy, and patients who survive typically develop chronic lung and kidney disease.

    (Choice A) Bladder outlet obstruction (eg, posterior urethral valves) causes bladder distension, bilateral hydronephrosis, and in severe cases impaired kidney function and oligohydramnios. However, in contrast to this case, renal cysts would not be present.

    (Choice B) Cerebral aneurysms are a common complication of autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease. However, most patients remain asymptomatic until adulthood; ARPKD is much more likely in this neonate with severe renal impairment.

    (Choice C) Segmental facial hemangiomas may be associated with other anomalies, as in the PHACE syndrome (abnormalities of the Posterior fossa; Hemangiomas; and Arterial cerebrovascular, Cardiac, and Eye anomalies). Vascular tumors are not associated with ARPKD or Potter sequence.

    (Choice D) Intestinal atresia is associated with excessive, not reduced, amniotic fluid (polyhydramnios) due to impaired fluid absorption by the fetal gastrointestinal tract. Furthermore, this patient's renal findings would not be expected in a patient with intestinal atresia.

    Educational objective:

    Amniotic fluid is largely composed of fetal urine, and oligohydramnios (low fluid) is often associated with kidney dysfunction in the fetus (eg, diffuse cysts seen in autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease). Extrinsic fetal compression from a low amniotic fluid level leads to Potter sequence with characteristic findings of flattened facies, limb deformities, and pulmonary hypoplasia.
