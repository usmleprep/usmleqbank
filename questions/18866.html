An 80-year-old woman presents to the office for evaluation of progressively worsening urinary incontinence. The patient reports that she is leaking urine "all of the time" and is using the bathroom multiple times at night. She loses urine immediately after she has the urge to void and often cannot make it to the bathroom. The patient was recently diagnosed with mild cognitive impairment but maintains independence with the assistance of her husband. She has no other medical problems. Vital signs are normal. Pelvic examination shows vaginal atrophy but is otherwise normal. There is no leaking of urine with cough or Valsalva maneuver. Postvoid residual volume and urinalysis are normal. A medication with which of the following pharmacodynamic effects would be most helpful in treating this patient's urinary incontinence?

A. Antagonism of alpha-1 adrenergic receptors
B. Antagonism of beta-1 adrenergic receptors
C. Antagonism of beta-3 adrenergic receptors
D. Stimulation of beta-1 adrenergic receptors
E. Stimulation of beta-3 adrenergic receptors
F. Stimulation of muscarinic cholinergic receptors

    • Explanation

    The patient has urgency incontinence (or overactive bladder), which is caused by uninhibited bladder contractions (detrusor instability) that results in a sudden, strong urge to void followed immediately by loss of urine. Pharmacotherapy in these patients is aimed at either promoting detrusor relaxation (via beta-3 adrenoceptors) or inhibiting contraction (via muscarinic cholinergic receptors) with 2 main medication classes:

    • Beta-3 receptor agonists (eg, mirabegron), which causes detrusor smooth muscle relaxation, allow increased urine storage and decrease incontinent episodes. Because they are selective receptor agonists, they are often better tolerated with fewer systemic adverse effects.
    • Antimuscarinics (eg, oxybutynin), which block M3 receptors at the bladder, prevent detrusor contractions. However, antimuscarinics often act on several types of muscarinic receptors and should be used with caution, especially in elderly patients and those with cognitive impairment due to medication adverse effects (eg, decreased cognition, blurred vision, drowsiness).

    (Choice A) Alpha-1 blockers (eg, doxazosin, prazosin, terazosin) are used to treat benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) and hypertension. They relax the smooth muscle in arterial and venous walls, leading to decreased peripheral vascular resistance, and also relax the smooth muscle in the bladder neck and prostate, leading to decreased urinary obstruction in patients with BPH.

    (Choices B and D) Beta-1 adrenergic receptors are found in cardiac tissue and on renal juxtaglomerular cells. Selective beta-1 blockers (eg, metoprolol) decrease heart rate and contractility and block catecholamine-induced renin release by the kidney.

    (Choice C) In contrast to beta-3 adrenergic receptor agonists, antagonists would cause increased bladder spasms (ie, contractions) and increased urine loss.

    (Choice F) Muscarinic receptor activation leads to contractions of the bladder musculature and promotes bladder emptying. Cholinergic agonists (eg, bethanechol) are used to treat urinary retention or neurogenic bladder by strengthening muscular contractions. These medications would worsen this patient's symptoms.

    Educational objective:

    Urgency incontinence, or overactive bladder, is caused by uninhibited bladder contractions (detrusor instability) that results in a sudden urge to void followed by an involuntary loss of urine. Treatment with beta-3 adrenergic receptor activation (eg, mirabegron) relaxes the detrusor muscle, promotes urine storage, and decreases incontinent episodes.
