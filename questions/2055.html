    Palpitations are a subjective sensation of the heartbeat usually caused by rapid arrhythmia or forceful ventricular contraction. This patient with palpitations and an irregularly irregular pulse most likely has atrial fibrillation, the most common cause of an irregularly irregular rhythm.

    In atrial fibrillation, the atria experience rapid and chaotic electrical activity, and organized atrial depolarization is lost. In addition, because the rate of atrial electrical impulses is so high (eg, 500-600/min) and the atrioventricular node does not transmit impulses that quickly, impulses are transmitted to the ventricles randomly and intermittently. Therefore, ECG typically shows an absence of P waves and an irregularly irregular rhythm with varying R-R intervals; the ventricular rate is often (but not always) >100/min. In addition, irregular, low-amplitude fibrillatory waves are sometimes present between the QRS complexes.

    The development of atrial fibrillation is encouraged by atrial remodeling, which involves changes to atrial conduction pathways that occur as a result of aging and underlying chronic disease (eg, hypertension, heart failure). Once a certain degree of remodeling is present, acute illness (eg, sepsis or other causes of increased sympathetic tone) and other conditions (eg, hyperthyroidism) can initiate foci of electrical activity that trigger the arrhythmia. Excessive alcohol consumption is also a common trigger (ie, "holiday heart syndrome").

    (Choice A) Ventricular bigeminy involves a premature ventricular contraction after each normal sinus beat and is often triggered by periods of increased sympathetic tone. Palpitations are common, but the rhythm is regularly irregular (ie, R-R interval varies in a predictable manner), with 2 beats occurring close together followed by a relatively long pause before the next beat.

    (Choice B) Ventricular tachycardia demonstrates wide QRS complexes on ECG. It may cause palpitations, but the rhythm is regular.

    (Choice C) Atrial flutter demonstrates saw-toothed flutter waves on ECG and may cause palpitations. Because atrial activity is slower (eg, 300/min) and somewhat more organized than in atrial fibrillation, the rhythm can be regular or irregular, depending on the steadiness of conduction through the atrioventricular node. This ECG shows steady 2:1 conduction with a regular rhythm, which is not consistent with this patient's irregularly irregular pulse.

    (Choice E) Atrioventricular nodal reentrant tachycardia commonly causes palpitations, but the rhythm is regular. P waves are often not visible on ECG because they are buried within the QRS complexes.

    Educational objective:

    Atrial fibrillation is the most common cause of an irregularly irregular rhythm. It often presents with palpitations and is confirmed by ECG showing an absence of organized P waves and irregularly irregular R-R intervals.
