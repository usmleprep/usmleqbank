A 64-year-old woman is diagnosed with rheumatoid arthritis after coming to the office with symmetrical joint pain, swelling, and morning stiffness. The patient has a prior history of gastrointestinal bleeding from a peptic ulcer caused by Helicobacter pylori; she was treated appropriately and a follow-up H pylori stool antigen test was negative. The patient is started on methotrexate and high-dose ibuprofen. In addition, daily lansoprazole is prescribed for protection against the adverse gastrointestinal effects of ibuprofen. Three months later, joint symptoms are greatly improved, and the patient is maintained on methotrexate alone. Shortly thereafter, she begins experiencing heartburn after meals. Which of the following is the most likely cause of this patient's new gastrointestinal symptoms?

A. Downregulation of vagal stimulation
B. Gastrin-mediated parietal cell hypertrophy
C. Increased prostaglandin production after ibuprofen discontinuation
D. Proton pump inhibitor-induced gastric mucosal atrophy
E. Upregulation of somatostatin receptors

    • Explanation

    The release of gastrin, a hormone produced by G cells in the gastric antrum, is stimulated by dietary protein intake, gastrin-releasing peptide (released in response to vagal stimuli), and increased gastric pH. Gastrin increases acid production directly by binding parietal cells and indirectly by binding enterochromaffin-like (ECL) cells and inducing histamine release.

    Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) (eg, omeprazole, lansoprazole) inhibit the hydrogen-potassium-ATPase pump and directly decrease hydrochloric acid production by parietal cells regardless of the source of stimulation. The resultant increase in gastric pH leads to increased gastrin formation, which, over time, can lead to hypertrophy of ECL and parietal cells. After chronic use (eg, >8 weeks), abrupt cessation of the PPI can trigger rebound gastric acid hypersecretion by the hyperfunctioning parietal cells, leading to reflux symptoms; PPIs can be slowly tapered to prevent this adverse effect.

    (Choice A) Acetylcholine (released with vagal stimulation) binds to parietal cell muscarinic (M3) receptors and promotes the secretion of hydrochloric acid. Downregulation of vagal tone would likely improve, rather than cause, this patient's symptoms.

    (Choice C) Prostaglandins decrease acid production (by inhibiting histamine release) and increase gastric epithelial cell bicarbonate secretion. Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (eg, ibuprofen) inhibit prostaglandin formation and increase the risk of gastritis and peptic ulcer formation. Increased prostaglandin formation would decrease gastric acid secretion.

    (Choice D) Rebound gastric acid hypersecretion after chronic use of PPIs is due to parietal cell hyperplasia and enlargement rather than gastric mucosal atrophy.

    (Choice E) Somatostatin inhibits histamine and gastrin release; upregulation of somatostatin receptors would lead to reduced gastric acid formation.

    Educational objective:

    Elevated gastric pH stimulates secretion of gastrin, a polypeptide hormone that increases gastric acid production. Proton pump inhibitors block gastric acid production by parietal cells; the resultant increase in pH leads to hypergastrinemia, which can cause rebound hypersecretion of gastric acid when the drug is withdrawn after chronic use.
