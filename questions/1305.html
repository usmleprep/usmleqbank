A 24-year-old man comes to the office for hearing loss, tinnitus, and unsteadiness with walking. A contrast-enhanced, T1-weighted image of his brain is shown below.

This patient is most likely to have a mutation involving which of the following genes?

A. NF1 on chromosome 17
B. NF2 on chromosome 22
C. RB1 on chromosome 13
D. TP53 on chromosome 17
E. TSC1 on chromosome 9
F. VHL on chromosome 3

    • Explanation

    This patient has enhancing masses affecting both internal auditory canals with extension into the cerebellopontine angles, findings characteristic of vestibular schwannoma (VS). Also called acoustic neuromas, they typically cause hearing loss, tinnitus, and/or imbalance (true vertigo is uncommon). Bilateral VS are the hallmark of neurofibromatosis type 2 (NF2).

    NF2 is an autosomal dominant disorder caused by mutations in the NF2 gene on chromosome 22 that encodes for merlin, a tumor suppressor protein associated with the plasma membrane. Because merlin is expressed primarily in neural cells, loss of function results in multiple nervous system tumors. Schwann cells are most susceptible, giving rise to the most common tumor (schwannoma), and merlin's alternative name (schwannomin).

    Patients with NF2 can also develop schwannomas affecting other cranial and spinal nerves; central nervous system ependymomas and meningiomas also occur with increased frequency.

    (Choice A) Mutations of the NF1 tumor suppressor gene (chromosome 17) cause neurofibromatosis type 1 (NF1). Although NF1 can have CNS involvement (eg, optic pathway gliomas), bilateral VS are not characteristic. In addition, cutaneous manifestations (eg, caf√©-au-lait macules) are more common, and the peripheral nerve tumors of NF1 are neurofibromas (not schwannomas).

    (Choice C) Mutations of the RB1 tumor suppressor gene (chromosome 13) increase the risk for retinoblastoma and osteosarcoma.

    (Choice D) Mutations affecting the TP53 gene (codes for p53) on chromosome 17 are the most commonly acquired mutations in cancer. Inherited mutations cause the rare cancer-predisposing Li-Fraumeni syndrome (eg, breast cancer, sarcomas). Although affected patients frequently develop brain tumors at a young age, bilateral VS are much more suggestive of schwannomatosis (eg, NF2).

    (Choice E) Mutation of the TSC1 tumor suppressor gene (chromosome 9) or TSC2 (chromosome 16) leads to tuberous sclerosis complex, an inherited neurocutaneous disorder that can cause brain tumors. However, these are typically periventricular (subependymal nodules) and associated with multisystemic findings, including skin (eg, facial angiofibromas, ash-leaf macules), eye, and heart manifestations.

    (Choice F) Mutation of the VHL tumor suppressor gene (chromosome 3) causes von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) disease. Although VHL can cause cerebellopontine angle masses (endolymphatic sac tumors), other CNS (eg, hemangioblastomas) and visceral (eg, clear renal carcinomas, pheochromocytomas) tumors are common.

    Educational objective:

    Bilateral vestibular schwannomas are the hallmark of neurofibromatosis type 2, a dominantly inherited disorder caused by mutations of the NF2 gene on chromosome 22.
