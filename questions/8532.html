An infant is born with facial dysmorphia and a cleft palate. Chest x-ray shows decreased soft tissue density in the right anterior mediastinum. Cardiac imaging reveals a right-to-left shunt. The infant experiences frequent and recurrent sinopulmonary infections but never develops appreciable lymphadenopathy. The cells deficient in this patient's condition are normally the major constituent of which of the following lymph node regions?

A. Germinal center
B. Medullary cords
C. Medullary sinuses
D. Paracortex
E. Primary lymphoid follicle

    • Explanation

    This patient with craniofacial abnormalities, recurrent sinopulmonary infections, and congenital heart disease most likely has a 22q11 deletion syndrome (22qDS). The various 22qDS phenotypes, including DiGeorge syndrome, are caused by loss of genes responsible for embryonic transformation of the pharyngeal apparatus (eg, head and neck structures) and the secondary heart field (eg, cardiac outflow tract). The third pouch is commonly affected, typically manifesting as thymic hypoplasia (eg, attenuation of the thymic shadow on chest x-ray).

    The thymus is the site of T-cell maturation, and its maldevelopment precludes the normal migration of mature T cells to secondary lymphoid organs. In lymph nodes, mature T cells are found in high number within the paracortex (a region lying internal to the cortex, between the follicles and medulla). Normally, this region will enlarge significantly during cell-mediated immune responses due to proliferation of cytotoxic and helper T cells. The loss of mature T reservoirs in patients with 22qDS results in recurrent infections by viral, fungal, protozoan, and intracellular bacterial pathogens.

    (Choices A and E) B cells mature in the bone marrow before migrating to the periphery; in the lymph nodes, they cluster in the outer cortex as primary (inactive) follicles. When exposed to antigens (aided by follicular T cells and dendritic cells), B cells begin proliferating and form a germinal center. In x-linked agammaglobulinemia, B cells are unable to mature in the bone marrow, preventing migration and resulting in absent lymph node cortical follicles.

    (Choices B and C) The medulla of the lymph node consists of medullary cords (aggregates of reticular cells, plasma cells, and macrophages) and medullar sinuses.

    Educational objective:

    22q11.2 microdeletion syndromes (eg, DiGeorge syndrome) may present with thymic hypoplasia (eg, thymic shadow attenuation). Because the thymus is required for T-cell maturation, reservoirs that receive migrating mature T cells (eg, paracortex of lymph nodes) will be poorly developed. In contrast, x-linked agammaglobulinemia halts B-cell maturation in the bone marrow, preventing migration to lymph node cortical follicles.
