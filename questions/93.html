A 78-year-old man is admitted to the hospital due to fatigue, orthopnea, and exertional dyspnea. The patient takes no medications and has not seen a physician in many years. He is a lifelong nonsmoker. Blood pressure is 110/60 mm Hg and pulse is 64/min. Examination reveals bibasilar crackles, elevated jugular venous pressure, and bilateral lower extremity pitting edema. Left ventricular diastolic pressure-volume curve is shown in the exhibit (purple curve).

Which of the following is the most likely cause of this patient's condition?

A. Alcohol use
B. Doxorubicin therapy
C. Selenium deficiency
D. Transthyretin deposition
E. Viral myocarditis

    • Explanation

    This patient's fatigue, exertional dyspnea, and orthopnea as well as lung crackles, jugular venous distension (JVD), and lower extremity edema on physical examination are consistent with decompensated heart failure. The left ventricular (LV) diastolic pressure-volume curve shows elevated pressure at any given LV volume. This finding indicates reduced LV compliance (ie, diastolic dysfunction), as occurs in conditions causing heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (eg, hypertensive heart disease, restrictive cardiomyopathy).

    Cardiac amyloidosis is a common cause of restrictive cardiomyopathy resulting from abnormal accumulation of amyloid proteins, particularly immunoglobulin light chains or transthyretin (protein carrier of thyroxine and retinol). Transthyretin amyloidosis most often affects the heart of elderly patients and lead to an infiltrative restrictive cardiomyopathy. The ventricular walls become thickened and stiff and unable to relax during diastole; higher pressures are required to fill the ventricle. Elevated hydrostatic pressure backs up into the lungs (pulmonary edema) and into the right side of the heart and systemic venous system (JVD and peripheral edema).

    LV contractility is intact with infiltrative restrictive cardiomyopathy (especially early in the disease process), but it can decrease as the disease progresses, eventually leading to LV cavity dilation and a picture consistent with dilated cardiomyopathy.

    (Choices A, B, C, and E) Excessive alcohol use, doxorubicin chemotherapy, selenium deficiency, and viral myocarditis are all potential causes of dilated cardiomyopathy; they do not cause restrictive cardiomyopathy. Dilated cardiomyopathy is characterized by LV cavity enlargement and reduced LV contractility leading to systolic dysfunction with reduced ejection fraction. LV wall compliance is typically normal or increased.

    Educational objective:

    Reduced ventricular compliance is seen with restrictive cardiomyopathy and other causes of diastolic dysfunction and heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (eg, hypertensive heart disease). It involves ventricular wall thickening and/or stiffening that leads to increased diastolic filling pressure at any given ventricular volume.
