He applies sunscreen before going outside, but often forgets to reapply it during prolonged outdoor periods. He states that he sunburns easily and has never been able to suntan like his peers. The patient has otherwise been healthy. Temperature is 37.1 C (98.8 F), blood pressure is 110/70 mm Hg, pulse is 90/min, and respirations are 20/min.     Melanin, the pigment responsible for hair shade and skin tone, is synthesized in specialized organelles (ie, melanosomes) within melanocytes and released to pigment-containing cells in the skin, hair, and eye through a series of steps:

    1. Fibrils form within premelanosomes, creating a scaffold that will bind and concentrate the melanin pigment within the organelles.
    1. Endosomes within the melanocyte then transport enzymes (eg, tyrosinase) to premelanosomes.
    1. Tyrosinase catalyzes the first step (ie, oxidation of tyrosine to DOPA) of melanogenesis, leading to the production of black-brown eumelanin and yellow-red pheomelanin.
    1. Melanized melanosomes are transferred via dendritic branches into neighboring keratinocytes, where they are then arranged around the cell nucleus to protect the DNA.

    This patient with diffuse lack of pigmentation and light sensitivity likely has oculocutaneous albinism (OCA), a condition characterized by impaired production or distribution of melanin, particularly eumelanin. Isolated OCA is most often caused by autosomal recessive mutations causing dysfunctional melanin synthesis within melanosomes (most commonly tyrosinase or P protein [regulates melanosome pH]), resulting in low or often completely absent melanin production.

    Characteristic findings of OCA include diffuse white hair and skin and light irides. Photophobia is common because translucent irides allow in excess light. Because eumelanin protects against ultraviolet damage, patients with OCA are predisposed to sunburns, as in this boy. Patients are also at increased risk for skin cancer, particularly squamous cell carcinoma.

    (Choice A) The pathogenesis of vitiligo is multifactorial but likely involves CD8+ lymphocyte‚Äìmediated melanocyte apoptosis. Patches of depigmentation are characteristic; although the patches can coalesce to cause universal depigmentation, the process is gradual, chronic, and highly unlikely by age 10.

    (Choice B) Formation of premelanosomes occurs normally in OCA. Melanized melanosome numbers may be decreased, which is secondary to reduced melanin production and not the primary mechanism of pigmentation defects in OCA.

    (Choice C) Uptake of melanin by keratinocytes is disrupted in Ch√©diak-Higashi syndrome due to a defect in organelle trafficking. Patients have OCA, but recurrent pyogenic infections and coagulopathy would also be expected in early childhood.

    (Choice E) Impaired differentiation of neural crest cells into melanocytes is a rare cause of scattered areas of depigmentation in piebaldism and Waardenburg syndrome. A white forelock is characteristic but is not seen in this patient.

    Educational objective:

    Oculocutaneous albinism is most commonly due to mutations that cause dysfunctional melanin synthesis within melanosomes. Patients typically have complete absence of melanin, resulting in white skin and hair, light irides, photophobia, and a propensity for sunburns.
