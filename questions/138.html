A 55-year-old man comes to the office due to recurrent episodes of retrosternal chest pain. The episodes occur during physical activity, usually when he climbs stairs or walks uphill, and resolve after he rests for a few minutes. The patient has no known medical conditions and does not use tobacco, alcohol, or recreational drugs. He is prescribed sublingual nitroglycerin to take as needed when the chest pain occurs. This drug is most likely to relieve this patient's symptoms by causing which of the following hemodynamic changes?

A. Decrease in heart rate
B. Decrease in left ventricular end-diastolic volume
C. Decrease in peripheral arteriolar resistance
D. Increase in coronary blood flow
E. Increase in coronary perfusion pressure

    • Explanation

    This patient's retrosternal chest pain brought on by exertion and relieved with rest is classic for stable angina. It is caused by atherosclerotic narrowing of the coronary arteries such that an adequate increase in myocardial blood flow (and oxygen supply) cannot be achieved to meet the increased myocardial oxygen demand of exercise. Medical therapy for stable angina is aimed at relieving this myocardial oxygen supply/demand mismatch.

    Nitrates are commonly used to treat stable angina. Long-acting formulations (eg, isosorbide dinitrate) are used for prevention of stable angina, and short-acting formulations (eg, sublingual nitroglycerin) are used as an abortive agent when symptoms occur. The primary effect of nitrates is venodilation, which causes additional blood to remain in the capacitance veins and reduces venous return to the heart. The resulting decrease in left ventricular (LV) end-diastolic volume (ie, reduced preload) decreases LV wall stress to reduce myocardial oxygen demand and relieve angina symptoms.

    (Choice A) Decreased heart rate reduces myocardial oxygen demand. Beta blockers (eg, metoprolol) and nondihydropyridine calcium channel blockers (eg, verapamil) treat stable angina in part via this mechanism, but nitrates have no direct effect on heart rate (and may cause a reflexive increase in heart rate).

    (Choice C) Decreased peripheral arteriolar resistance decreases cardiac afterload to reduce LV wall stress and decrease myocardial oxygen demand. Dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers (eg, amlodipine) treat stable angina via this mechanism, but nitrates cause only slight peripheral arteriolar vasodilation.

    (Choices D and E) Nitrates do stimulate some degree of coronary arteriolar vasodilation, but this mechanism has little effect on relieving angina symptoms. Coronary arterioles with substantial atherosclerotic disease are already nearly fully dilated at baseline such that the capacity for further dilation to recruit additional blood flow is minimal. Nitrates can also slightly augment the coronary perfusion pressure gradient (ie, aortic diastolic pressure ‚àí LV diastolic pressure) by decreasing LV end-diastolic volume. This may slightly increase coronary blood flow and myocardial oxygen supply, but the reduction in myocardial oxygen demand via decreased LV wall stress is a much larger contributor to relief of angina symptoms.

    Educational objective:

    Sublingual nitroglycerin is used for rapid symptom relief in patients with stable angina. The primary anti-ischemic effect of nitrates is mediated by venodilation causing a decrease in left ventricular end-diastolic volume and wall stress, resulting in decreased myocardial oxygen demand.
