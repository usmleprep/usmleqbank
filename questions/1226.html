A 32-year-old man with HIV is diagnosed with pulmonary tuberculosis and started on 4-drug combination therapy. During a follow-up visit a month later, the patient reports red urine and red staining of his contact lenses. A sputum sample is obtained and grows Mycobacterium tuberculosis isolates with resistance to several antimycobacterial drugs. Bacterial resistance to the agent responsible for this patient's current side effects most likely developed through which of the following mechanisms?

A. Altered structure of enzymes involved in bacterial DNA winding-unwinding
B. Altered structure of enzymes involved in bacterial RNA synthesis
C. Altered structure of bacterial ribosomal proteins
D. Decreased activity of bacterial catalase-peroxidase
E. Upregulation of bacterial beta-lactamase synthesis

    Most cases of pulmonary tuberculosis are initially treated with a combination of 4 drugs (RIPE therapy):

    • Rifamycins (eg, rifampin, rifabutin)
    • Isoniazid
    • Pyrazinamide
    • Ethambutol

    Although each of these medications can cause adverse effects, harmless red-orange discoloration of body fluids (eg, urine, sweat, saliva, tears) is typically caused by a rifamycin. Rifamycins exert their effect by blocking DNA-dependent RNA polymerase, which prevents bacterial transcription and subsequent protein synthesis; resistance typically arises due to mutations that result in structural alterations of the enzyme.

    (Choice A) Structural alteration of enzymes involved in DNA winding-unwinding would impair the activity of fluoroquinolones, which bind and inhibit DNA gyrase in bacterial cells, causing fracture of the DNA chain. Fluroquinolones do not cause reddish discoloration of body fluids.

    (Choice C) Antibiotics that act on the ribosome can be divided into those that act on the 50S subunit (eg, chloramphenicol, clindamycin, linezolid, macrolides) and those that act on the smaller 30S subunit (tetracycline, doxycycline, aminoglycosides). These are not typically used in initial 4 drug therapy for TB.

    (Choice D) Decreased bacterial catalase-peroxidase activity is a mycobacterial resistance mechanism for isoniazid. Before it can inhibit mycolic acid synthesis, isoniazid must be converted to its active form by the mycobacterial catalase-peroxidase enzyme. However, isoniazid does not cause reddish discoloration of body fluids.

    (Choice E) Upregulation of beta-lactamase synthesis would impair the activity of beta-lactam drugs (eg, penicillins, aminopenicillins, cephalosporins, monobactams). Infections caused by organisms expressing beta-lactamase require the addition of a beta-lactamase inhibitor (eg, clavulanic acid, sulbactam, tazobactam) or the use of an alternate drug class (eg, carbapenem).

    Educational objective:

    Rifamycins (eg, rifampin) block the action of the bacterial DNA-dependent RNA polymerase, thereby inhibiting bacterial transcription; resistance is acquired by mutations that modify the enzyme's structure. Harmless red-orange discoloration of body fluids is a common adverse effect.
