A 6-month-old girl is brought to the clinic for a routine care appointment. The patient has had no illnesses, although the mother says that she has trouble latching when breastfeeding. She can roll over when prone but cannot roll from the supine position or sit unsupported. The patient was born vaginally after an uncomplicated pregnancy. Vital signs are within normal limits. On physical examination, she has a head circumference above the 97th percentile for age and a full anterior fontanelle. At birth, the patient's head circumference was at the 50th percentile. The remainder of the physical examination is unremarkable. CT scan of the head reveals significant dilation of the lateral ventricles. If this patient's condition is left uncorrected, which of the following complications is most likely to develop?

A. Lower extremity hyporeflexia
B. Medulloblastoma
C. Meningitis
D. Muscle spasticity
E. Subarachnoid hemorrhage

    This patient has delayed gross motor milestones, an enlarging head circumference, and ventriculomegaly on imaging, findings consistent with hydrocephalus. Hydrocephalus is caused by the abnormal accumulation of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) in the brain. In infants, it is usually due to impaired drainage of CSF from either structural malformation (eg, aqueductal stenosis, Chiari malformations, Dandy-Walker syndrome) or postinfectious fibrosis (eg, cytomegalovirus, toxoplasmosis, meningitis); it can also be due to obstruction resulting from intraventricular hemorrhage.

    Infants with hydrocephalus typically have an enlarging head circumference (ie, macrocephaly) inappropriate to their height and weight; developmental delay often occurs and poor feeding is common. Classic features of increased intracranial pressure (eg, irritability, vomiting, papilledema) may not be present because the open fontanelle allows for volume expansion with less of an increase in pressure. Muscle spasticity and hyperreflexia result from upper motor neuron injury caused by stretching of the periventricular pyramidal tracts. Seizures can also occur.

    Diagnosis is confirmed with neuroimaging (eg, ultrasound, CT scan or MRI of the head). Treatment requires surgical placement of a shunt to bypass the obstruction, most often via the ventriculoperitoneal route.

    (Choice A) Lower extremity hyporeflexia would result from damage to the lower motor neurons; hydrocephalus impacts the upper motor neurons.

    (Choice B) A medulloblastoma can lead to compression of the cerebral aqueduct and result in obstructive hydrocephalus that spares the fourth ventricle. However, hydrocephalus does not predispose to development of medulloblastoma.

    (Choice C) Meningitis can lead to fibrosis that obstructs the flow of CSF and leads to hydrocephalus. Patients with implanted shunt devices are also at increased risk for meningitis. However, untreated hydrocephalus does not increase the risk for meningitis.

    (Choice E) Subarachnoid hemorrhage can cause inflammation and resulting fibrosis of the arachnoid granulations, leading to impaired resorption of CSF. Therefore, subarachnoid hemorrhage can be a cause, but not a consequence, of hydrocephalus.

    Educational objective:

    Congenital hydrocephalus often presents with macrocephaly and poor feeding. Imaging studies showing enlarged ventricles are diagnostic. Untreated hydrocephalus leads to developmental delays, poor growth, and muscle spasticity (due to stretching of the periventricular pyramidal tracts).
