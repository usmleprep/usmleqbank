A 42-year-old, previously healthy man comes to the office for evaluation of an enlarging mole. The mole has been on his forearm for several years but has rapidly increased in size over the past 4 months. Physical examination shows an 8-mm raised lesion with variegated pigmentation and ill-defined borders on the right forearm. An excisional biopsy is performed. During the procedure, local anesthesia is achieved with an infiltrating injection of lidocaine mixed with epinephrine. Which of the following is the most likely reason for adding epinephrine to the injection?

A. Altering nociceptive fiber neurotransmission
B. Decreasing pain during subcutaneous infiltration
C. Increasing anesthetic efficacy via a synergistic effect
D. Increasing anesthetic potency by decreasing tissue pH
E. Prolonging the duration of action of the anesthetic agent

    • Explanation

    Lidocaine is a local anesthetic that blocks pain transmission from free nerve endings. It is often mixed with epinephrine, which causes profound vasoconstriction when injected subcutaneously (alpha-1 agonist effect). This effect results in the following:

    • Decreased local bleeding, making the procedure easier to complete
    • Decreased systemic absorption of lidocaine, reducing the potential for serious adverse effects (eg, arrhythmia, seizure)
    • Prolonged duration of action, because decreased blood flow allows more lidocaine to remain at the injection site

    (Choice A) Although epinephrine acts as a catecholamine neurotransmitter in the CNS, it mediates autonomic functions in the peripheral nervous system. Glutamate and substance P are the main neurotransmitters of pain sensation in the peripheral nervous system.

    (Choices B and D) Local anesthetics are weak bases that exist in charged and uncharged forms; the uncharged form is promoted by increased tissue pH and allows the anesthetic to diffuse through the cell membrane and bind the inside portion of sodium channels to prevent depolarization. Sodium bicarbonate is often added to lidocaine injections to increase tissue pH, which increases the potency of lidocaine.

    (Choice C) Medications that interact synergistically produce a greater cumulative effect than if the effects of the 2 agents alone were added together (eg, pain relief with an opioid + acetaminophen). Epinephrine does not produce anesthesia, so although it prolongs the duration of anesthesia, it does not act synergistically to increase the anesthetic effect.

    Educational objective:

    Epinephrine is often added to lidocaine to produce vasoconstriction, which prolongs the duration of action of lidocaine, decreases bleeding during a procedure, and reduces systemic lidocaine absorption.
