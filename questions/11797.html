A 16-year-old previously healthy boy is brought to the office due to palpitations. He was watching a documentary at a movie theater when his heart suddenly began to race and he became lightheaded. The patient says that he has felt "rapid heartbeats" in the past and thought that they were due to stress from school examinations, but he has never had associated lightheadedness before. He has no chest pain or dyspnea. Physical examination is within normal limits. Resting ECG is shown in the exhibit.

Which of the following is most likely present in this patient?

A. Accessory bypass tract connecting the atria and ventricles
B. Cardiac conduction system degeneration below the level of the atrioventricular node
C. Ectopic atrial foci that serve as the dominant pacemaker
D. Impaired generation of impulses in the sinoatrial node
E. Prolonged ventricular action potential due to myocyte ion channel abnormality

    • Explanation

    This patient's resting ECG shows the characteristic Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) pattern, which consists of the following triad:

    • Short PR interval, generally <120 msec
    • Slurred and broad upstroke of the QRS complex (delta wave)
    • QRS widening, often with ST segment/T wave changes

    These findings are created by an accessory pathway (bundle of Kent), which allows ventricular depolarization to begin before the normal depolarization impulse can pass through the atrioventricular node (ie, ventricular preexcitation). Many patients with WPW pattern on ECG remain asymptomatic, but some develop paroxysmal tachyarrhythmias (ie, episodes of palpitations and lightheadedness); these patients are considered to have WPW syndrome.

    Atrioventricular reentrant tachycardia (AVRT), a form of paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia, is the most common arrhythmia seen with WPW syndrome. It results when a premature atrial or ventricular contraction creates a reentrant circuit involving the accessory pathway and the atrioventricular node. Notably, the characteristic findings of WPW pattern are not evident on ECG during AVRT and are best demonstrated during normal sinus rhythm.

    (Choice B) Cardiac conduction system degeneration below the level of the atrioventricular node (eg, His Purkinje system) leads to variable degrees of conduction block and typically presents with symptomatic bradycardia.

    (Choice C) Patients with wandering atrial pacemaker or ectopic atrial rhythm have ectopic atrial foci that replace the sinus node as the dominant pacemaker; P waves of varying morphology are seen on ECG. This patient has normal, uniform P waves suggesting normal sinus node function.

    (Choice D) Impaired generation of impulses in the sinoatrial node can occur with sick sinus syndrome, which usually affects elderly patients. It appears on ECG as a delayed P wave or the absence of a P wave with the QRS complex formed by a junctional escape beat.

    (Choice E) Congenital long QT syndrome is caused by mutations in the genes encoding cardiac sodium or potassium channels. The prolonged repolarization interval (eg, QTc > 450 msec) puts patients at risk for developing polymorphic ventricular tachycardia (eg, torsades de pointes).

    Educational objective:

    Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) pattern is characterized by a shortened PR interval, slurred initial upstroke of the QRS complex (delta wave), and widening of the QRS complex. It is caused by an accessory pathway that bypasses the atrioventricular node, leading to ventricular preexcitation. Some patients develop paroxysmal tachyarrhythmias (eg, atrioventricular reentrant tachycardia) due to reentry of electrical impulses through the accessory conduction pathway (ie, WPW syndrome).
