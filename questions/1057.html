A 50-year-old woman, gravida 0 para 0, comes to the office for an annual checkup. She has no family history of cancer. Breast examination is normal. Screening mammography reveals microcalcifications, and the patient undergoes breast biopsy. Histopathology reveals ducts distended by pleomorphic cells with prominent central necrosis. The lesion does not extend beyond the ductal basement membrane. Which of the following is the most likely site of origin of this patient's lesion?

B. B

    • Explanation

    The breast is composed of stromal and epithelial elements:

    • Stroma consists of fibrous connective tissue and adipose tissue and constitutes the majority of breast volume in nonlactating adult women.
    • Epithelial elements include lobes that connect via branching ducts to lactiferous sinuses, which drain to the nipple. These elements proliferate during pregnancy in preparation for lactation.

    The lobes consist of terminal duct lobular units (TDLUs), the epithelial elements that serve as the functional units of the breast. Each TDLU comprises a lobule (formed by acini) and its associated terminal duct. Most breast carcinomas arise from the TDLU, including both ductal and lobular carcinomas, which likely originate from a common cell lineage but have distinct molecular and biologic features. Both types typically begin as in situ carcinomas that can be distinguished histologically:

    • Ductal carcinoma in situ: Histopathology typically demonstrates ducts and lobules containing neoplastic epithelial cells that fill the lumens; when lobules are involved, the cells typically distend the acini, imparting a "ductal" appearance. Some cases have pleomorphic cells with central necrosis, which can lead to dystrophic calcifications (often detected on mammography).
    • Lobular carcinoma in situ: Histopathology typically demonstrates ducts or lobules containing monomorphic neoplastic cells that appear discohesive (due to decreased E-cadherin expression).

    Penetration of the ductal or lobular basement membrane signifies transition from in situ to invasive breast carcinoma.

    (Choice A) Lipomas are benign tumors that arise from subcutaneous fat. They characteristically form a soft, mobile mass that remains stable or slowly enlarges. When the tumor is excised, histopathology shows encapsulated mature adipocytes.

    (Choice C) Phyllodes tumors arise from the breast stroma and can be malignant. Histopathology shows leaf-like architecture due to proliferation of cellular stromal connective tissue that distorts the epithelium.

    (Choice D) Spread of malignant cells through the duct system to the nipple can result in the eczematous nipple changes (eg, redness, scaling, ulceration) characteristic of Paget disease. Histopathology typically shows large adenocarcinoma cells with irregular nuclei and prominent nucleoli within the epidermis.

    Educational objective:

    The terminal duct lobular unit is the functional unit of the breast and the site of origin for most breast carcinomas. Ductal carcinoma in situ is characterized by neoplastic epithelial cells (sometimes with pleomorphism and central necrosis) that fill the ducts/lobules.
