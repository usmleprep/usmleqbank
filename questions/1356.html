A 65-year-old man comes to the office due to vision difficulties. He says his eyesight has gotten progressively worse over the past year. The fundus of the patient's left eye is shown in the exhibit.

Which of the following defects is most likely to be revealed by visual field testing?

A. Arcuate scotoma
B. Binasal hemianopia
C. Bitemporal hemianopia
D. Central scotoma
E. Homonymous hemianopia

    • Explanation

    This older patient with vision difficulties has yellow deposits (drusen) clustered around the macula (central portion of the retina), a concerning sign of age-related macular degeneration (AMD). Lesions that affect the macula can lead to loss of a portion of the central visual field, which is referred to as a central scotoma.

    The macula is located near the center of the retina. It is a region densely packed with cone photoreceptors with very few overlying cells and no blood vessels. Each cone cell in the macula synapses to a single bipolar cell, which synapses to a single ganglion cell (in contrast to the peripheral retina, where multiple cones converge onto a single ganglion cell). This 1:1 arrangement is why the macula (and particularly the fovea) conveys greater visual acuity than any other area in the retina.

    AMD primarily affects the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE), which contains the glial support cells that provide nutrients for retinal neurons and eliminate waste products. With RPE degeneration, waste products (eg, drusen) accumulate, leading to the death of overlying macular neurons and subsequent central vision loss. Loss of vision in this area is particularly debilitating, and AMD is the most common cause of blindness in patients >age 50 in the United States.

    (Choice A) An arcuate scotoma is an arc-shaped visual field defect that extends from the blind spot (the area of the optic nerve head unable to process visual information) and follows the bending course of the retinal nerve fibers. It classically develops due to glaucoma (which often causes peripheral loss of vision); funduscopic examination often shows an increased cup/disc ratio.

    (Choice B) Binasal hemianopia is loss of vision in the nasal fields of both eyes. This information travels at the lateral edge of the optic chiasm and does not cross within it. For a lesion to produce binasal hemianopia, it would have to affect the lateral optic chiasm on both sides. The fundus would be unaffected.

    (Choice C) Bitemporal hemianopia is loss of vision in the temporal fields of both eyes. Fibers from the nasal retina cross the midline at the optic chiasm, so lesions that compress the center of the optic chiasm (eg, pituitary adenoma) can cause bitemporal hemianopia. Funduscopic changes would not occur.

    (Choice E) Homonymous hemianopia is loss of vision in the same side of the visual field in both eyes. It occurs with lesions posterior to the optic chiasm and does not lead to changes in the appearance of the fundus.

    Educational objective:

    A scotoma is a discrete, usually monocular, visual field defect. Central scotomas are often caused by age-related macular degeneration; funduscopy classically reveals deposition of drusen around the macula.
