A 22-year-old man comes to the office for an exercise evaluation. He is an avid long-distance runner. One of his team members was disqualified recently due to health issues, which made him worried about his own health. He has no medical history and takes no medications or recreational drugs. Blood pressure is 125/75 mm Hg and pulse is 55/min. Examination shows no abnormalities. Transthoracic echocardiogram results are as follows:

Left atrium mildly enlarged
Left ventricular (LV) cavity size small
LV septal thickness increased
LV posterior wall thickness normal
LV mass increased
LV ejection fraction 70%

The right ventricle appears normal, and there are no valvular lesions. Which of the following pathologic findings are most likely to be present within this patient's heart?

A. Accumulation of amorphous substance in interstitial space
B. Distorted myocardial structure and myofibril disarray
C. Increase of individual myocardial fiber length more than width
D. Myocardial atrophy and replacement with fibrous fatty tissue
E. Subendocardial replacement fibrosis

    This patient most likely has hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM), an autosomal dominant inherited condition resulting from mutations in the cardiac sarcomere and leading to distorted myocardial structure and myofibril disarray. Recognition of HCM is important because it is one of the most common causes of sudden cardiac death (SCD) in young adults. Although patients may experience exertional dyspnea, chest pain, lightheadedness, or syncope, they are often asymptomatic.

    Echocardiographic findings consistent with HCM include:

    • Overall increase in left ventricular (LV) mass
    • Reduced LV cavity size (with impaired diastolic function)
    • Asymmetric increase in LV wall thickness predominantly affecting the septum
    • Normal or increased LV ejection fraction
    • Left atrial enlargement (secondary to increased LV end-diastolic pressure)

    Another characteristic of HCM is a poorly developed coronary capillary network in hypertrophied regions with evidence of chronic ischemia (ie, fibrosis, scarring).

    (Choice A) Accumulation of amorphous substance in the cardiac interstitial space occurs with cardiac amyloidosis, which usually affects older patients (eg, age >60). In early disease, a restrictive pattern develops, with stiff and thickened LV walls leading to diastolic dysfunction. LV cavity size remains normal.

    (Choice C) An increase in individual myocardial fiber length more than width (ie, eccentric hypertrophy) occurs with athlete's heart, constituting physiologic adaptation to high-level endurance training. There is enlarged LV cavity size and a small degree of uniformly increased LV wall thickness. The changes of athlete's heart must be differentiated from those associated with pathologic disease; this patient's reduced LV cavity size and localized septal wall thickening are red flag features suggesting underlying pathology due to HCM.

    (Choice D) Myocardial atrophy and replacement with fibrous fatty tissue occur with arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy, an autosomal dominant condition involving mutations affecting cardiac desmosomes. The disease primarily affects the right ventricle, causing ventricular arrythmias and sometimes right ventricular failure. When the left ventricle is involved, it commonly demonstrates eccentric enlargement with increased LV cavity size.

    (Choice E) Subendocardial replacement fibrosis can occur following viral myocarditis and can lead to dilated cardiomyopathy. LV contractile function is disrupted, causing increased LV cavity size and reduced LV ejection fraction.

    Educational objective:

    Patients with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) may be asymptomatic. Echocardiographic findings consistent with HCM include an overall increase in left ventricular (LV) mass, reduced LV cavity size with impaired diastolic function, LV hypertrophy predominantly affecting the septum, and normal or increased LV ejection fraction.
