A 14-year-old girl is brought to the office due to a bump on her chest just below the right breast. The bump has been there as long as she can remember, but it became a little larger 2-3 years ago. The bump also becomes tender just before her menses start. Menarche was at age 12. Menses occur every 28 days and last 4-5 days. Examination shows Tanner stage 5 breasts. Both breasts and axillae appear normal. There is a soft, raised, hyperpigmented, 0.5-cm nontender lesion inferior to the right breast. Which of the following is the most likely cause of this patient's presentation?

A. Adipocyte proliferation within a fibrous capsule
B. Clonal proliferation of benign melanocytes
C. Failed involution of the mammary ridge
D. Pedunculated outgrowth of normal skin
E. Sunlight-induced production of melanin

    • Explanation

    This patient's hyperpigmented papule that is inferior to the breast and becomes tender before menses is most likely an accessory nipple (ie, polythelia, supernumerary nipple).

    In utero, the breasts develop from the mammary ridge (milk line), which is an ectodermal thickening that initially extends from the axilla to the medial thigh bilaterally; the remainder of the mammary ridge subsequently regresses in early gestation. Incomplete involution of the mammary ridge results in an accessory nipple (sometimes with associated glandular tissue) anywhere along this ridge. The most common location of an accessory nipple is inferior to the breast, as seen in this patient.

    Accessory nipples are usually asymptomatic, well-circumscribed, hyperpigmented papules. They may become larger during puberty, before menses, or with pregnancy/lactation due to rising estrogen and progesterone (similar to physiologic changes in normal breast tissue). Associated glandular tissue may also become tender or swollen, as seen in this patient. The diagnosis is clinical, and no treatment is generally required.

    (Choice A) A lipoma is a benign tumor characterized by adipocyte proliferation within a fibrous capsule. It typically presents as a soft, painless, subcutaneous nodule with normal overlying epidermis. This patient's hyperpigmentation and tenderness make this diagnosis unlikely.

    (Choice B) A congenital melanocytic nevus is a clonal proliferation of benign melanocytes presenting as hyperpigmentation that is well-demarcated and may be flat or raised. However, a congenital melanocytic nevus would be painless; premenstrual swelling/tenderness is more characteristic of accessory breast tissue.

    (Choice D) An acrochordon (skin tag) is a pedunculated outgrowth of normal skin that often develops near skinfolds (eg, axilla, beneath breasts) due to friction. Hyperpigmentation with tenderness before menstruation would not be seen.

    (Choice E) An ephelis (freckle) is a hyperpigmented macule that occurs due to sunlight-induced production of melanin by melanocytes. An isolated lesion beneath the breast (not sun-exposed) is atypical, and freckles are not raised or tender.

    Educational objective:

    Incomplete involution of the mammary ridge during fetal development can lead to an accessory nipple, a well-circumscribed, hyperpigmented papule located anywhere from the axilla to the thigh. Similar to hormone-induced changes in normal breast tissue (eg, due to puberty/menses), they may grow larger and become tender/swollen.
